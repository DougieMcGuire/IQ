The question "can you increase your IQ?" has a complicated answer in cognitive science. The short version: your baseline fluid intelligence is partly genetic, but your cognitive performance — including on IQ tests — absolutely improves with the right interventions.

The Science of IQ Malleability

For decades, IQ was considered essentially fixed. But research over the past 20 years has overturned this view significantly. Key findings:

  • Jaeggi et al. (2008) showed that n-back working memory training produced significant gains in fluid intelligence — the core component of IQ
  • Childhood nutrition, education quality, and cognitive stimulation affect adult IQ by up to 20 points
  • The Flynn Effect demonstrates average IQ rising 3 points per decade — entirely due to environmental factors
  • Bilingualism is associated with a 4–7 point IQ advantage and delayed cognitive decline

What Actually Increases IQ Performance

1. Daily Cognitive Training (Biggest Short-Term Gain)

Structured practice on the specific types of tasks measured by IQ tests produces the largest short-term gains. Pattern recognition, working memory, mental math, and verbal reasoning practice all improve performance on IQ assessments. Consistent daily training for 4–8 weeks produces measurable improvements.

2. Aerobic Exercise (Best Long-Term Investment)

Regular cardiovascular exercise produces structural brain changes — increased hippocampal volume, higher BDNF levels, more efficient neural connections. Studies consistently show 2–5 IQ point advantages for people who exercise regularly versus sedentary peers.

3. Sleep Optimization (Most Neglected Factor)

Sleep is when memory consolidation happens. Chronic sleep deprivation reduces IQ-test performance by 5–8 points. Getting 7–9 hours of quality sleep is arguably the single most impactful "IQ booster" most people aren't taking seriously.

4. Learning a Complex Skill

Learning to play a musical instrument, speaking a second language, or mastering a complex game like chess produces broad cognitive gains that transfer to IQ-measured abilities. These activities train multiple cognitive systems simultaneously.

5. Nutrition (Omega-3s, Creatine, Iron)

Omega-3 fatty acids support neural function and are associated with higher IQ scores in children and better cognitive performance in adults. Iron deficiency causes cognitive impairment that reverses with supplementation. Creatine supplementation shows modest positive effects on working memory in sleep-deprived individuals.

6. Stress Reduction

Chronic stress physically damages the prefrontal cortex — the brain region responsible for the executive function measured by IQ tests. People under chronic stress score significantly lower on IQ assessments. Reducing stress may "unlock" cognitive capacity that was previously suppressed.

What Doesn't Increase IQ (Despite Popular Claims)

  • Brain training games alone — improve performance on the specific games but show limited transfer without combining with other interventions
  • Most nootropic supplements — modest effects at best, mostly in people with deficiencies
  • Listening to Mozart — the "Mozart Effect" was largely debunked; any short-term effect is due to arousal, not musical content
  • Speed reading courses — don't improve comprehension, just reduce it at high speeds
